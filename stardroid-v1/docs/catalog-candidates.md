# Sky Map Catalog Candidates

Objects proposed for addition or completion. Priority given to naked-eye and binocular targets.

---

## Category 1: Meteor Shower Info Cards (HIGH PRIORITY)

All 10 existing showers are already **rendered on the map** as radiant points, but **none have info
cards**. This is the most impactful quick win — no code or catalog changes needed, just content.

| Shower | Peak rate | Peak date | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Perseids | 100/hr | Aug 13 | Most popular shower of the year |
| Geminids | 120/hr | Dec 14 | Best annual shower; source is asteroid 3200 Phaethon |
| Quadrantids | 120/hr | Jan 4 | Short, sharp peak; often overlooked |
| Eta Aquariids | 70/hr | May 6 | Debris of Halley's Comet; best from southern hemisphere |
| Orionids | 25/hr | Oct 21 | Also Halley's Comet debris |
| Leonids | 20/hr | Nov 18 | Famous for historical storms (1833, 1966, 1999) |
| Lyrids | 18/hr | Apr 22 | Oldest recorded shower (687 BC) |
| Delta Aquariids | 16/hr | Jul 30 | Good from southern latitudes |
| Puppid-Velids | 10/hr | Dec 7 | Southern hemisphere focus |
| Ursids | 10/hr | Dec 23 | Circumpolar; observable on Christmas Eve |

## Category 2: Saturn Moon System (virtual objects)

The Galilean moons and Mars's moons are already implemented as virtual objects. Saturn's major
moons should follow the same pattern. Titan is a serious binocular target.

| Moon | Magnitude | Visibility | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Titan** | **8.5** | **Binoculars** | Largest moon; atmosphere; only moon with thick nitrogen air |
| **Rhea** | **10.0** | Small telescope | Second largest; heavily cratered ice ball |
| **Tethys** | **10.3** | Small telescope | Odysseus crater; Ithaca Chasma rift valley |
| **Dione** | **10.4** | Small telescope | Ice cliffs; possible subsurface ocean |
| **Iapetus** | **10.0–11.9** | Small telescope | Two-toned: one hemisphere coal black, one snow white |
| Enceladus | 11.7 | Telescope | Famous cryo-geysers; liquid water ocean; possible life |
| Mimas | 12.9 | Telescope | Herschel crater gives it Death Star appearance |

**Recommended first pass:** Titan, Rhea, Tethys, Dione, Iapetus (these five are the classic "bright
five" of Saturn's system).

## Category 6: Additional meteor showers to consider adding to the layer

The layer currently requires >10/hr. The Taurids are notable for fireballs despite low rate, and
the Draconids can outburst unpredictably.

| Shower | Peak rate | Peak date | Case for adding |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Southern Taurids** | ~5/hr | Nov 5 | Famous for slow, bright fireballs; visually spectacular despite low rate |
| **Northern Taurids** | ~5/hr | Nov 12 | Complement to S. Taurids; combined produce the "Halloween fireball season" |
| **Draconids** (Giacobinids) | ~2/hr normally | Oct 8 | Can spectacularly outburst (10,000+/hr in 1933, 1946); parent comet is 21P/Giacobini-Zinner |
| Alpha Monocerotids | rare | Nov 21 | Occasional intense outbursts (1995, 2019, 2022) |

## Suggested prioritization

1. **Meteor shower info cards** — 10 objects already on map, zero content. Biggest impact per effort.
2. **Omega Centauri and 47 Tucanae info cards** — brightest objects on the map with no cards.
3. **Titan as a virtual object** — first Saturn moon; establishes the Saturn moon series.
4. **Hyades cluster** — brightest missing cluster; glaring absence.
5. **Helix + Sculptor Galaxy** — bright missing DSOs.
6. **Remaining bright map objects without cards** (M47, M41, M24, M25, M39, etc.)
7. **Remaining Saturn moons** (Rhea, Tethys, Dione, Iapetus).
8. **Remaining missing DSOs** (planetaries, NGC 2903, etc.)
9. **Triton and Uranian moons** (telescope-only; lower casual appeal).
10. **Taurids/Draconids** — layer changes needed, evaluate separately.
